Few places on Earth hit you the way Torres del Paine does on that first clear morning — the sky an impossible blue, three granite needles catching the sun above a milk-jade lake, condors circling on thermals overhead. It is the kind of landscape that makes you feel small in the best possible way.

The park sits in Chilean Patagonia, roughly 150 km north of Puerto Natales, and protects one of the most dramatic mountain massifs on the planet. The Paine Massif is a young intrusion of quartzite and granite, pushed up through older sedimentary rock and then carved into towers, horns, and hanging glaciers by the ice ages. The result looks like a stage set — almost too extravagant to be real.

## The W and O Circuits

The park offers two signature multi-day routes:

**The W Trek** (4–5 days, ~80 km) traces the three valleys that form the shape of a W. It connects the three major highlights of the park — the towers, the French Valley, and Grey Glacier — in a single continuous route. Moderate fitness required; the trails are well-marked and serviced by a chain of refugios.

**The O Circuit** (8–10 days, ~130 km) adds a full loop around the back of the massif, connecting via the remote northern side and crossing Paso John Gardner — at 1,241 m, the highest point of the trek with jaw-dropping views over the Southern Patagonian Ice Field. Significantly fewer people, more demanding terrain, and weather that is famously cruel.

Both routes share the same W-shaped section. Most hikers do the W; fewer continue for the O. I did the full O and would do it again in a heartbeat.

## Day by Day: The W Trek

### Day 1 — Mirador Las Torres

**Trailhead → Campamento Torres → Mirador Las Torres and back → Refugio Las Torres**
*~19 km · 900 m elevation gain · 8–9 h*

Start at the Refugio Las Torres complex. The first hour follows a gentle valley — easy walking, good for warming up. At Campamento Torres the trail changes character: a steep boulder-field scramble to the mirador that takes 45 minutes of real effort.

Then the lake opens in front of you. Three walls of quartzite, nearly vertical, reflected perfectly in a cirque lake that exists because a glacier scraped this hollow and then melted. The colour of the water changes through the day — milky at noon, deep teal in the afternoon, almost black at dawn.

**Tip:** Book the campamento Torres for an overnight so you can be at the lake at sunrise (around 06:30 in summer) before day-hikers arrive. The difference is enormous.

### Day 2 — Las Torres to Los Cuernos

**Refugio Las Torres → Refugio Los Cuernos**
*~11 km · moderate · 4–5 h*

A pleasant day along Lago Nordenskjöld with the horns of Los Cuernos across the water. The Cuernos are among the most photographed features of the park — sedimentary dark caprock sitting on top of lighter granite, giving them their distinctive banded appearance.

Short day by design. Use the afternoon to dry gear, eat well, and rest for Day 3.

### Day 3 — Valle del Francés

**Refugio Los Cuernos → Campamento Italiano → Mirador Británico → Campamento Italiano → Refugio Paine Grande**
*~26 km · 900 m elevation gain · full day*

The physical crux of the W. The valley hike to Mirador Británico is steep and relentless, but the payoff is the best 360° mountain panorama of the entire trek. You are surrounded: Paine Grande (3,050 m) to the left, Los Cuernos to the right, a glacier-carved amphitheatre of hanging ice, avalanche tracks, and waterfalls straight ahead.

Leave your full pack at Campamento Italiano — only a daypack is allowed into the valley. Then push on to Paine Grande in the afternoon.

**Tip:** The section from Italiano to Paine Grande follows the lakeside — deceptively long. Leave Mirador Británico by 14:00 at the latest if you want to reach Paine Grande before dark.

### Day 4 — Glaciar Grey

**Refugio Paine Grande → Refugio Grey → Mirador Glaciar Grey and back**
*~22 km · 500 m elevation gain · 7–8 h*

The westernmost day and the most other-worldly. Lago Grey is full of icebergs calved from the glacier — some the size of a car, some the size of a house. The trail skirts the lake before climbing to a viewpoint where the full width of the glacier comes into view: 6 km wide, cracked into pressure ridges, slowly creeping toward the water.

Guided ice treks on the glacier itself are available from Refugio Grey. Worth it if the schedule allows.

**Return:** Take the catamaran from Paine Grande back to park administration, or continue with the O Circuit from Grey.

## The O Circuit Extension

For those adding the back loop, Days 5–9 cross to the northern side of the massif via Campamento Serón, Dickson, and Los Perros before the big day: the Paso John Gardner crossing.

### Paso John Gardner

The pass earns its reputation. The climb from Campamento Los Perros is brutal — steep, often muddy, frequently horizontal rain and wind. Trekking poles are essential. The descent on the far side is loose scree.

But if the weather clears — and occasionally, dramatically, it does — you look west over the Southern Patagonian Ice Field, the third largest ice mass on Earth. Glaciers in every direction, as far as you can see, flowing down from a plateau that has barely changed since the last ice age. There is nothing else like it on a walking trail anywhere in the world.

After the pass, the route drops to Campamento Paso and then continues south to reconnect with the W at Refugio Grey.

## Practical Information

### Permits and Booking

CONAF (Chile's forestry service) manages the campsites; the refugios and some private campsites are run by Fantastico Sur and Vertice Patagonia. **Book everything before you arrive in Chile** — the park has strict daily limits and the most popular spots (Campamento Torres, Grey) sell out 6–12 months in advance.

CONAF entrance fee: ~USD 35 for foreigners. Park is open year-round but the high season (December–February) is best for stable weather.

### Getting There

1. Fly into Punta Arenas (PMC) — about 3 h from Santiago.
2. Bus or transfer to Puerto Natales (~3 h by road).
3. Bus or transfer to park entrance — either Laguna Amarga (for the eastern Las Torres trailhead) or Pudeto (for the catamaran to Paine Grande on the western side).

### Best Season

| Season | Conditions |
|--------|------------|
| Dec–Feb | Long days, warmest, most crowded |
| Mar–Apr | Autumn colours, fewer people, still stable ★ |
| Oct–Nov | Spring, more wind, fewer crowds |
| May–Sep | Short days, snow possible, many refugios closed |

March–April is the sweet spot. The forest turns gold and red, the light is dramatic, and you share the trail with a fraction of the summer crowds.

### Gear

Patagonia weather changes in minutes. You need:

- **Waterproof jacket and pants** (not just water-resistant — proper waterproof)
- **Trekking poles** — mandatory for Paso John Gardner, very helpful everywhere
- **Warm layers** — temperatures can drop below freezing even in summer
- **Gaiters** — the trail gets muddy, especially on the back side of the O
- **Headlamp** — for pre-dawn starts to the towers
- **Dry bags** — protect sleeping bag and electronics

The refugios sell basic supplies at park prices (expensive). Bring all your snacks and lunches from Puerto Natales.

## The Part That Stays With You

On the last morning of the O Circuit, coming down from Grey toward Paine Grande with the glacier behind me and the sun finally out after two days of rain, I sat on a rock and ate the last of my chocolate and just looked.

The light in Patagonia has a quality I cannot fully explain — something about the latitude and the absence of pollution and the sheer scale of everything. The mountains look like they are lit from inside.

You plan a trip like this around logistics — permits, campsites, weather windows. You go home carrying something harder to name. For me it has become a kind of measure: when something difficult comes up in ordinary life, I think *you crossed the pass in horizontal rain and finished the loop*. That tends to put things in perspective.

If you are thinking about it: go.
